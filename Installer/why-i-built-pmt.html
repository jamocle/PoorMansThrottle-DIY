Poor Man’s Throttle

Why I Built PMT

A low-cost wireless throttle system created to make model train control more accessible, more approachable, and less expensive for the people who love this hobby.

If Poor Man’s Throttle has helped you, inspired you, saved you money, or made this hobby more accessible, I would sincerely appreciate your support.

@Jamocle

Poor Man’s Throttle was never built by a company, a funded startup, or a team with a budget. It was built by one person who saw a problem in the model train community and decided to do something about it. Me.

This project is a true team of one. Every part of it comes from me: the firmware, the app code, the engineering, the documentation, the videos, the testing, the troubleshooting, the updates, and the support that help make it real and usable.

I created Poor Man’s Throttle because I know how expensive this hobby can be. I know how quickly costs add up. I know what it feels like to want a better way to run trains, only to find that the commercial answer comes with a price tag that puts it out of reach. For many hobbyists, branded throttles and proprietary systems are simply too expensive to justify.

That never sat right with me.

I believed there should be another path. A practical path. An affordable path. A path for hobbyists who are willing to build, learn, and create, but should not have to spend a small fortune just to enjoy smooth wireless control. I believed there should be a way to take accessible hardware, thoughtful engineering, and careful design and turn them into something people could actually use.

That belief became Poor Man’s Throttle.

On the surface, it may seem simple: smartphone control, an ESP32, an inexpensive motor driver, and a low-cost way to wirelessly run a locomotive. But anyone who has ever built something meaningful knows the truth. What looks simple on the outside is often the result of a tremendous amount of unseen work underneath. Simplicity takes time. Simplicity takes iteration. Simplicity is earned.

I did this so you would not have to.

Poor Man’s Throttle did not appear overnight. It was not a quick experiment that happened to work. It came from a long, ongoing investment of time, energy, persistence, and care. It took engineering. It took testing. It took false starts, wrong turns, revisions, rethinking, and rebuilding. It took time to make the hardware practical, the control experience useful, and the documentation clear enough for others to follow.

When people see a project page, a working app, a firmware build, or a video demonstration, they see the result. What they do not always see is the mountain of work behind it. They do not see the hours spent debugging stubborn issues, refining code, rewriting explanations, improving instructions, or trying to make the next version better than the last. They do not see how much effort it takes to make something feel approachable to someone seeing it for the first time.

But that work is real. In fact, it is where most of the labor lives.

I built the firmware so others would not have to start from scratch. I worked on the app so others could have a usable interface instead of just an idea. I developed the engineering approach so others could follow a path that had already been thought through. I wrote the documentation so others would not have to guess their way forward. I created supporting material and offered help because a project is only truly useful when people can actually succeed with it.

I did this because I wanted to give something back to the model train community.

This hobby has always been full of builders, tinkerers, makers, and people who simply love trains enough to keep learning. But even the most passionate hobbyist has limits, and one of the biggest limits for many people is cost. Commercial systems can be excellent, but they are not always accessible. For some hobbyists, they are out of reach entirely. For others, they are hard to justify when there are already so many other expenses competing for attention.

That is why this project matters to me. Poor Man’s Throttle is not just about saving money, though saving money matters. It is about making capability more accessible. It is about proving that you should not need an expensive proprietary ecosystem just to enjoy better control, wireless operation, and a more modern experience.

For dead-rail builders, this project offers a low-cost wireless path using accessible components and common battery options. For traditional DC users, it offers a meaningful improvement through PWM motor control and smartphone operation. For the wider DIY model train community, it represents something even bigger: a way forward that is understandable, flexible, and affordable.
