This is a Team of One

This project is a true team of one. Every part of it comes from me: the firmware, the app code, the engineering, the design decisions, the documentation, the testing, the troubleshooting, the videos, the updates, the support, and all of the behind-the-scenes effort it takes to make something like this real, usable, and worth sharing.

I created Poor Man’s Throttle because I know how expensive this hobby can be. I know how quickly costs stack up. I know what it feels like to want something better for a layout or a locomotive, only to find that the commercial answer comes with a price tag that makes you stop in your tracks. For many people, branded throttles and proprietary wireless systems are simply too expensive to justify. And for a hobby built on creativity, building, problem-solving, and passion, that kind of barrier can feel especially discouraging.

That never sat well with me.

Why This Exists

I believed there should be another path. A practical path. An affordable path. A path for the hobbyist who is willing to build, learn, experiment, and create, but should not have to spend a small fortune just to get smooth wireless control. I believed there should be a way to take accessible hardware, solid engineering, and careful design and turn them into something the average person could actually use.

That belief became Poor Man’s Throttle.

At a glance, it may seem simple: smartphone control, an ESP32, an inexpensive motor driver, and a low-cost way to wirelessly run a model locomotive. But anyone who has ever built something meaningful knows the truth — what looks simple on the surface is often the result of a tremendous amount of unseen work underneath. Simple is expensive in hours. Simple is earned through trial and error. Simple is what happens after someone chooses to wrestle with the hard parts long enough to spare everyone else from them.

I did this so you would not have to.

Why It Matters

This hobby has always been full of resourceful people — builders, tinkerers, makers, problem-solvers, and people who simply love trains enough to keep learning. But even the most passionate hobbyist has limits, and one of the biggest limits for many people is cost. Commercial systems can be excellent, but they are not always accessible. For some hobbyists, they are out of reach entirely. For others, they are hard to justify when there are locomotives to maintain, track to buy, batteries to source, tools to replace, and a hundred other expenses already competing for attention.

That is why this project matters to me.

Poor Man’s Throttle is not just about saving money, though saving money matters. It is about making capability more accessible. It is about proving that you should not need an expensive proprietary ecosystem just to enjoy smoother low-speed control, wireless operation, and a more modern experience. It is about opening a door for the hobbyist who wants a better solution but needs one that is grounded in affordability and practicality.

For dead-rail builders, this project offers a low-cost wireless control path that works with accessible components and common battery options. For traditional DC users, it provides a powerful improvement by allowing the transformer to stay at full power while the throttle hardware uses PWM motor control for smoother operation, better starting torque, and improved low-speed performance. For the broader DIY model train community, it represents something even bigger: a way forward that is understandable, flexible, and not locked behind premium pricing.
