PMT Possibilities

Think Outside The Battery.

PMT is not limited to a single power strategy. These four operating scenarios show how it can work with track power, onboard battery power, or a hybrid of both, giving users more flexibility to match the way they actually run their layout.

Traditional conversations around control systems often begin and end with onboard batteries, but PMT opens the door to more than one approach. Depending on the layout, locomotive, and operating style, users can choose a setup that prioritizes simplicity, wireless control, independent train operation, powered accessories, or full deadrail freedom.

The point is choice. These scenarios show that PMT can fit into a wide range of layouts without forcing a single way of thinking about power. Whether you want constant track voltage, control inside the locomotive, battery-only operation, or a best-of-both-worlds hybrid, the system can be adapted to fit the experience you want.

Scenario 1

PMT between transformer and track

In this setup, the transformer is left at a constant 100% output and PMT sits between the transformer and the track. PMT becomes the control point for all track power and sends modulated power to the rails, while the smartphone provides wireless control over speed, direction, and braking.

Simple central control The transformer stays fixed at 100%, while PMT handles the actual control of track power.
Works with unmodified trains The image highlights that all trains on the track can be controlled by PMT without needing changes inside the locomotive.
Wireless smartphone operation Speed, direction, and braking can be managed from anywhere on the layout.
Clean transition path This is an easy way to add wireless control while keeping a familiar track-powered layout.
Best for users who want modern wireless control with minimal disruption to a conventional transformer-and-track setup.
Scenario 2

PMT installed in the locomotive with track power

Here the transformer still powers the rails at 100%, including accessories, buildings, and all trains on the track. But PMT is installed inside the locomotive, where it takes power from the rails and directly controls the motor and lights.

Track power remains useful The rails still feed accessories, buildings, and the rest of the layout at full power.
Locomotive-level control PMT inside the locomotive directly manages motor speed, direction, braking, and onboard lights.
Independent multi-train operation The image notes that multiple trains can be run independently on the same track.
Balanced flexibility This approach blends the familiarity of powered rails with more advanced per-locomotive control.
A strong option for layouts that still rely on powered track infrastructure but want smarter control inside each engine.
Scenario 3

Deadrail operation with onboard battery only

In this deadrail scenario there is no transformer and no power on the rails. PMT is installed in the locomotive and powered by an onboard battery, giving the locomotive its own self-contained source for motor control and lighting.

No rail power required The rails remain unpowered, which simplifies operation where clean electrical pickup is a concern.
Runs anywhere The image emphasizes that no transformer is required, making the setup more portable and self-contained.
Individual train control Multiple trains can be controlled independently on the same track.
True battery-driven freedom Motor and lighting control come directly from the onboard battery and PMT inside the locomotive.
Ideal for users who want complete independence from track power and the freedom of true deadrail operation.
Scenario 4

Transformer and battery hybrid

This hybrid model keeps the transformer supplying full power to the track for passenger cars, accessories, and other powered devices, while the locomotive itself is isolated from the rails and operated from an onboard battery through PMT.

Best of both worlds The image explicitly describes this as track power plus wireless engine control.
Powered accessories stay active Full rail power can continue supporting lighted cars, buildings, and trackside devices.
Locomotive isolated from the rails The engine runs from its onboard battery through PMT, independent of rail pickup.
Complete wireless engine control Throttle, direction, braking, and lights all remain under PMT control.
Perfect for layouts that want powered accessories and traditional rail-fed extras without giving up battery-based locomotive control.

Why this matters

These four scenarios make the same point from different angles: PMT is flexible. It can act as a bridge between a transformer and the rails, a controller inside the locomotive, a deadrail battery system, or the heart of a hybrid layout. That means users are not boxed into a single architecture or forced to redesign everything around one power philosophy.

More choice
Users can select the setup that fits their layout, not the other way around.
More compatibility
PMT can support conventional, deadrail, and hybrid operating styles.
More freedom
Wireless control can be paired with the power strategy that makes the most sense.
